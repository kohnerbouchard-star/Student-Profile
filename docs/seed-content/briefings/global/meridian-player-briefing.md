## You have arrived

You are a new immigrant in one of Econovaria's ten countries.

You came looking for opportunity.

You may have come for work, education, safety, family, investment, or the chance to build a business. You have limited money, temporary status, one local contact, and no established reputation.

Your first goals are personal:

- find reliable work;
- pay your expenses;
- build savings;
- learn how your new country works;
- develop useful relationships;
- improve your legal and professional position;
- create a future for yourself.

You have arrived during a major economic expansion known as the Meridian boom.

## Why people are coming

The ten countries are considering a shared infrastructure project called the Meridian Corridor.

The Corridor could connect:

- ports;
- rail lines;
- energy systems;
- commodity terminals;
- digital customs records;
- payment verification;
- data routes;
- research and maintenance systems.

Even before a final agreement has been reached, companies and governments are preparing.

They are hiring workers, financing projects, buying materials, expanding logistics, and competing for skilled people.

That creates opportunity for newcomers.

It also raises rent, increases competition, and makes the future of many businesses depend on decisions that have not yet been made.

## The ten-country economy

Each country specializes in different things.

- Northreach supplies strategic minerals, energy, and northern logistics.
- Yrethia manages regulated shipping, customs, and maritime insurance.
- Thaloris provides repair, bonded storage, and flexible alternate routes.
- Solvend develops advanced technology and research systems.
- Eldoran supplies food, commodities, and central distribution.
- Valerion provides clean energy, water systems, and green finance.
- Lumenor provides diplomacy, education, public information, and institutional coordination.
- Xalvoria provides large-scale finance and project management.
- Dravenlok supplies steel, rail equipment, machinery, and industrial capacity.
- Syndalis supplies cybersecurity, payment systems, and data infrastructure.

This specialization creates wealth, but it also creates dependence.

A port delay can stop food or machinery. A mineral restriction can slow technology production. A poor harvest can raise prices in countries far from Eldoran. A cyber incident can make cargo and payment records unreliable.

No country is fully self-sufficient.

## What the Corridor promises

The project could:

1. Reduce the cost and time required to move goods.
2. Create alternate routes when one system is disrupted.
3. Increase demand for workers and businesses.
4. Improve customs and payment verification.
5. Expand construction, technology, energy, finance, and industrial activity.
6. Support shared emergency systems.
7. Create new opportunities for immigrants and young workers.

## What the Corridor risks

The project could also:

1. Create large debts.
2. Give financiers or operators control over important assets.
3. Make countries dependent on one security or data provider.
4. Raise domestic prices when food, energy, housing, or materials become scarce.
5. Concentrate economic growth in a small number of cities and routes.
6. Exclude small businesses that cannot meet new standards.
7. Increase political conflict over ownership, migration, data, and national control.
8. Spread disruption quickly when connected systems fail.

## Why the countries disagree

The Corridor is not only an engineering project.

It is a decision about:

- who pays;
- who owns;
- who receives jobs and contracts;
- who controls data;
- who bears financial risk;
- how foreign workers and investors are treated;
- what happens during an emergency;
- whether countries can leave or revise the agreement.

Every country wants the benefits.

They do not agree on the price, the rules, or who should have control.

## Your position

You are not a president or military commander.

You are beginning as an ordinary economic participant in your adopted country.

Depending on your opportunities, you may become:

- an employee;
- entrepreneur;
- investor;
- trader;
- contractor;
- analyst;
- public servant;
- journalist;
- community leader;
- broker between countries.

Your influence should grow through work, evidence, relationships, reputation, and economic decisions.

You will not automatically control national policy.

## What you will do

During the opening stage, you will:

- learn about your adopted country;
- choose or receive an economic path;
- earn and manage money;
- meet local and international characters;
- evaluate Meridian opportunities;
- compare financing and governance proposals;
- observe how events affect prices, jobs, companies, and people;
- decide whom to trust;
- respond to information that may be incomplete;
- build a personal position before the world becomes more dangerous.

## The warning signs

The world is not at war when the standard campaign begins.

However, several pressures are increasing:

- food-price concerns;
- port congestion;
- strategic export reviews;
- water and energy pressure;
- skilled-worker shortages;
- cyber incidents;
- political hostility;
- stockpiling;
- stronger review of foreign residents and investors.

These signs may appear gradually.

The same event may create opportunity for one player and hardship for another.

## The story may become a war story

A major attack against the Meridian transport, customs, payment, or data system may become the spark for open conflict.

The first reports may be incomplete or contradictory.

You may see:

- damaged infrastructure;
- disrupted payments;
- border controls;
- rising prices;
- job losses and emergency hiring;
- changes in company values;
- pressure on foreign-born residents;
- requests from people you know;
- accusations that are later corrected.

The game is not mainly about becoming a soldier.

It is about trying to build and protect a life while the economy and society change around you.

## Opportunity during crisis

War and emergency create new work and business opportunities.

You may find opportunities in:

- logistics;
- food distribution;
- repair;
- cybersecurity;
- finance;
- manufacturing;
- analysis;
- public service;
- reconstruction;
- community assistance.

A profitable choice may still carry legal, social, or moral costs.

When evaluating an opportunity, ask:

1. What can I gain?
2. Who may bear the cost?
3. How could this decision return later?

## Belonging

As the crisis grows, your adopted country may ask more from you.

Your legal status, foreign relationships, travel, employment, or financial transfers may receive greater attention.

At the same time, someone connected to your former home may need help.

You may eventually need to decide:

- where you belong;
- whom you trust;
- what loyalty means;
- how much security you will exchange for opportunity;
- whether wealth is worth the obligations attached to it.

## What is confirmed at the beginning

- Lumenor has announced the Starfall Meridian Forum.
- All ten countries have been invited.
- Several financing and governance models are being discussed.
- Companies and institutions are preparing for expected investment.
- No final route, financing plan, ownership structure, or security model has been approved.
- The standard campaign begins before open war.

## What remains uncertain

- which countries will participate;
- who will control the Corridor;
- which risks will become crises;
- who will benefit most from the boom;
- how foreign workers and investors will be treated;
- whether the Corridor will unite the countries or divide them;
- who will be responsible if a major attack occurs;
- what kind of person you will become.

## Confirmed fact, forecast, allegation, and opinion

### Confirmed fact

A condition supported by current records or an authorized announcement.

### Forecast

An estimate of what may occur later.

### Allegation

A claim that someone is responsible, made before sufficient confirmation.

### Opinion or recommendation

A judgment about what should be done.

Strong decisions keep these categories separate.

## First tasks

Your first tasks should establish both personal and world stakes.

1. Review your adopted-country profile.
2. Identify your immediate financial objective.
3. Meet your first local contact.
4. Choose or receive an initial work, training, or business opportunity.
5. Complete `contract.meridian.evaluate-corridor.v1` after you understand how the Corridor affects your position.

Your Corridor assessment should identify:

- opportunities for your adopted country;
- personal opportunities available to you;
- economic and political risks;
- groups that may gain or lose;
- one important unanswered question.

# Country Immigrant Opening Framework v1

Stable content ID: `framework.player.country-immigrant-opening.v1`
Content type: narrative design framework
Version: 1.0.0-draft
Maturity: draft
Owner domain: narrative and player experience
Scope: reusable design rules; all player-specific state remains game-session scoped
Implementation status: content definition only

## Purpose

Define the structure every country-specific immigrant opening must follow so the player experiences the ten-country world through work, money, relationships, residency, and personal risk rather than detached exposition.

The framework preserves one shared campaign spine while allowing each country to feel economically and socially distinct.

## Shared opening promise

Every standard campaign begins with the player:

- arriving in one adopted country;
- holding temporary legal status;
- possessing limited local funds;
- knowing one local contact;
- receiving one credible path toward work, training, trade, investment, or entrepreneurship;
- carrying one obligation connected to their former home;
- having no established reputation or institutional authority;
- believing the Meridian boom creates a rare chance to build a fortune.

The opening must establish hope before large-scale conflict.

## Country-opening structure

Each country opening must define the following stages.

### Stage 1: Arrival

The player encounters the adopted country through an ordinary civilian arrival point such as:

- immigration processing;
- a rail or port terminal;
- employer housing;
- a university intake office;
- a community settlement center;
- a commercial district;
- a regional transport hub.

The scene should communicate:

- the country’s economic identity;
- one visible opportunity;
- one visible inequality or constraint;
- one immediate personal need;
- one unresolved question about belonging.

### Stage 2: Stabilization

The player must secure basic stability through supported systems or narrative equivalents:

- housing;
- employment or contract income;
- ordinary expenses;
- local banking or payment access;
- transportation;
- identification or residency compliance;
- social connection.

Failure to stabilize should create recovery paths, not immediate campaign termination.

### Stage 3: First opportunity

The player receives at least three defensible economic paths.

Each path must differ in:

- risk;
- expected income;
- required skill or capital;
- institutional connection;
- exposure to future war conditions;
- relationship consequences.

No country should have one obviously correct opening profession.

### Stage 4: Meridian connection

The player’s work, business, study, investment, or community role becomes connected to the Meridian Corridor.

The connection should explain why the player learns about the larger world.

Examples:

- an employer receives a Corridor contract;
- a small business gains new customers;
- housing demand rises near a project site;
- imported equipment becomes essential;
- a bank offers project-linked credit;
- a port or rail operator expands;
- an institution recruits analysts or translators;
- a technology contractor needs skilled labor.

### Stage 5: Fracture

International tension reaches the player through ordinary consequences:

- delayed goods;
- higher rent or food prices;
- employer screening;
- currency pressure;
- changed insurance terms;
- residency-document reviews;
- discriminatory suspicion;
- public demonstrations;
- contradictory news;
- pressure on relationships across borders.

The player should still have agency during escalation.

### Stage 6: Attack and war

The Meridian attack and any following war must change the player’s established life.

At least one consequence must affect each layer:

- personal: relationship, housing, family, status, safety, or obligation;
- economic: income, prices, assets, credit, employment, supply, or market access;
- political: residency rules, border access, public trust, institutional demands, or legal restrictions.

The player is not automatically drafted into military command or given national authority.

### Stage 7: Wartime adaptation

Every country opening must provide multiple lawful and narratively meaningful routes through wartime conditions, such as:

- strategic manufacturing;
- logistics and distribution;
- food or energy security;
- public administration;
- journalism and verification;
- community support;
- financial risk management;
- technology and cybersecurity;
- reconstruction planning;
- cross-border mediation where legally available.

Illicit or abusive conduct may exist as an abstract narrative risk, investigation subject, or consequence. Content files must not provide operational instructions for wrongdoing.

### Stage 8: Belonging decision

The player eventually faces a conflict between personal advancement and competing obligations.

Possible pressures include:

- permanent residency or citizenship conditions;
- disclosure of foreign relationships;
- restrictions on remittances or travel;
- pressure to support a strategic employer;
- a request from someone in the former home;
- suspicion toward immigrants from an opposing bloc;
- an opportunity that benefits the player but harms their community;
- a choice between public trust and private loyalty.

### Stage 9: Reckoning

The opening must connect to separate personal and world outcomes.

The player may succeed personally while the country declines, or sacrifice wealth while helping the country recover.

## Required relationship roles

Each country opening must define at least four recurring personal anchors.

### Sponsor or settlement contact

Provides the player’s first practical foothold.

May offer:

- housing;
- documents;
- introductions;
- employment;
- cultural guidance;
- a debt or expectation.

The sponsor must have their own interests and must not function only as a tutorial device.

### Local friend

Shows everyday life beyond formal institutions.

The friend should:

- have an independent goal;
- disagree with the player sometimes;
- react to economic conditions;
- be affected by the war;
- remember meaningful choices.

### Rival immigrant or peer

Begins from a similar position but follows a different strategy.

The rival should not be automatically hostile. The relationship may become:

- competitive;
- cooperative;
- resentful;
- respectful;
- estranged;
- mutually dependent.

### Employer, client, investor, or institutional gatekeeper

Connects the player to opportunity and compromise.

This character may control:

- work access;
- capital;
- a recommendation;
- a license;
- a strategic contract;
- advancement;
- legal exposure.

### Optional additional roles

- journalist or investigator;
- community representative;
- residency or security official;
- landlord or housing organizer;
- teacher or mentor;
- person connected to the former home.

## Relationship-state principles

Relationships should track more than approval.

Recommended dimensions:

- trust;
- obligation;
- respect;
- dependence;
- ideological alignment;
- perceived loyalty;
- unresolved harm;
- willingness to provide help;
- willingness to share information;
- public association risk.

A single visible friendship score is not sufficient for the full design.

## Player-background integration

Country openings must support multiple player backgrounds without creating ten completely separate campaigns.

Background should change:

- the first opportunity;
- selected dialogue;
- one starting relationship;
- one skill or credential;
- one former-home obligation;
- selected eligibility conditions.

Background must not predetermine morality, political loyalty, or final ending.

## Economic-story requirements

Every country opening must identify:

- at least three initial income routes;
- one capital-intensive route;
- one low-capital recovery route;
- one route tied to a major local industry;
- one route exposed to international trade;
- one wartime route;
- one route vulnerable to war;
- one route that creates a meaningful public-versus-private trade-off.

Exact rewards, prices, and probabilities remain unapproved until a real simulation and staging review are completed.

## War-story requirements

The war must not flatten every country into the same experience.

Country variation should include:

- proximity to conflict;
- strategic importance;
- dependence on imports;
- exposure to sanctions or capital controls;
- labor demand;
- displacement pressure;
- public trust;
- infrastructure damage risk;
- political restrictions;
- reconstruction opportunity.

No country is assigned a permanent moral alignment.

## Required content hooks

Each opening must identify links to:

- country briefing;
- Meridian global arc;
- at least one existing institution;
- at least one existing lead character;
- starting Contracts;
- local news;
- first economic event;
- wartime event family;
- relationship follow-ups;
- personal ending conditions.

## Runtime boundaries

A future implementation requires authoritative ownership for:

- player background;
- adopted country;
- arrival stage;
- housing status;
- residency status;
- relationship instances;
- obligations;
- personal goals;
- war phase;
- personal ending state.

Reusable content files do not contain live player choices or relationship values.

## Validation

A country opening is not review-ready unless:

- the player has a credible reason to choose the country;
- the opening shows both opportunity and constraint;
- at least three viable economic paths exist;
- personal characters have independent goals;
- the Meridian connection is concrete;
- war changes the player’s established life;
- lawful wartime adaptation paths remain available;
- belonging creates a genuine conflict;
- personal and world outcomes are distinct;
- no unsupported runtime capability is represented as currently implemented;
- no immigrant group is treated as inherently suspicious, criminal, disloyal, or economically harmful;
- names and representation pass a separate review.

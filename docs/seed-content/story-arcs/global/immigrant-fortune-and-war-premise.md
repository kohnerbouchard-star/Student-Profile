# Immigrant Fortune and War — Core Player Premise

Stable content ID: `premise.player.immigrant-fortune-war.v1`
Content type: global player premise
Version: 1.0.0-draft
Maturity: draft
Owner domain: narrative and player experience
Scope: reusable definition; all personal and world progress remains game-session scoped
Implementation status: content definition only; player-background, residency, relationship, and war-state persistence remain unverified

## Core promise

The player enters Econovaria as a new immigrant with limited money, temporary status, one local contact, and a reason to build a new life.

They arrive during an international investment boom created by the proposed Meridian Corridor. Jobs are expanding, companies are hiring, markets are active, and ambitious newcomers believe they can build fortunes.

The player has arrived at exactly the right moment to succeed.

They have also arrived shortly before economic rivalry becomes war.

The story follows the player from arrival, through opportunity and social belonging, into a conflict that changes prices, jobs, borders, residency rules, relationships, and the moral meaning of wealth.

## Player point of view

The world is presented through the player's daily life rather than through detached geopolitical exposition.

The player learns about the ten-country system because:

- an employer depends on foreign materials;
- rent rises after migration into a safer region;
- a business loses access to a port;
- a family member needs money sent across a closing border;
- an investment gains from military procurement;
- a residency officer asks about foreign relationships;
- a friend is called into public service;
- a customer cannot afford an imported necessity;
- a news correction changes the apparent cause of an attack;
- an old decision returns as a wartime favor, accusation, or obligation.

Global events must reach the player through economic, personal, and political consequences.

## Starting condition

At the beginning of a standard campaign, the player has:

- a newly issued temporary residency status;
- a small balance in the adopted country's local currency;
- access to ECO only where an international settlement path is explicitly supported;
- basic housing or a temporary room;
- one sponsor, relative, employer, or community contact;
- one employment or commercial lead;
- one unresolved obligation connected to the player's former home;
- no established reputation;
- no automatic political influence;
- no knowledge of future war events.

The player chooses or receives a background that changes opportunities without determining morality.

Candidate backgrounds:

- skilled worker;
- student or trainee;
- family-sponsored immigrant;
- small-business hopeful;
- trade apprentice;
- displaced professional;
- investor with limited capital;
- political exile;
- technical specialist;
- logistics worker.

## Personal goals

The game should establish personal stakes before the global crisis escalates.

Early goals may include:

- secure reliable employment;
- pay rent and ordinary expenses;
- build an emergency fund;
- improve legal residency status;
- support a person or family outside the adopted country;
- gain professional credentials;
- establish a business;
- purchase useful property or equipment;
- build a trusted social network;
- earn permanent residency or citizenship.

The war becomes meaningful because it threatens the life the player has already begun to build.

## Story spine

The standard campaign uses one shared global spine with country-specific and player-specific variation.

### Act I: Arrival

The player enters the adopted country, completes orientation, receives temporary status, meets an initial contact, and chooses the first economic path.

Primary feelings:

- uncertainty;
- hope;
- unfamiliarity;
- urgency;
- possibility.

### Act II: The Meridian Boom

The Corridor proposal creates visible opportunity.

The player can pursue work, trade, investment, public contracts, professional advancement, or entrepreneurship. They meet recurring characters and begin forming a place in the adopted country.

This act must feel materially better than the later wartime period. Peace needs to have economic and emotional value before it is disrupted.

### Act III: The Fracture

The international system begins to strain.

Possible signs:

- mineral export reviews;
- port congestion;
- food-price pressure;
- labor shortages;
- cyber incidents;
- disputed cargo records;
- hostile political speeches;
- military procurement growth;
- foreign-worker scrutiny;
- capital movement and inventory stockpiling.

The same event must appear differently depending on the player's occupation, country, wealth, and relationships.

### Act IV: The Meridian Attack

A coordinated attack damages a major Meridian transport, customs, payment, or data node during a high-value shipment.

Consequences may include:

- civilian casualties;
- damaged infrastructure;
- missing cargo;
- payment and customs failure;
- communications interruption;
- emergency border controls;
- conflicting accusations;
- market and currency volatility.

Attribution is initially uncertain. Early evidence may be incomplete, manipulated, misunderstood, or deliberately planted.

The attack is the narrative spark. It does not instantly reveal a correct enemy or moral answer.

### Act V: Outbreak

Mobilization, retaliation, alliance obligations, border incidents, and economic restrictions turn the crisis into open war.

The player does not personally decide whether the war begins. Prior player and group choices may affect preparedness, public trust, severity, available routes, institutional legitimacy, and which claims are believed.

### Act VI: Fortune During War

The player must adapt.

Wartime opportunities include:

- emergency logistics;
- food distribution;
- strategic manufacturing;
- reconstruction;
- cybersecurity;
- currency protection;
- supply contracts;
- emergency lending;
- distressed assets;
- cross-border brokerage;
- informal trade;
- investigative work.

Every profitable path should identify the source of profit and the people who bear its cost.

The central question is not whether the player may become wealthy. The central question is what the player is willing to become in order to succeed.

### Act VII: Belonging

The adopted country may demand proof of loyalty or impose new conditions on foreign-born residents.

Possible pressures:

- enhanced residency review;
- disclosure of foreign contacts;
- restrictions on transfers or travel;
- strategic-service obligations;
- pressure to endorse wartime policy;
- security interviews;
- citizenship or permanent-status decisions;
- requests to renounce another affiliation.

At the same time, people connected to the player's former home may require assistance.

The player must decide what home, loyalty, and responsibility mean.

### Act VIII: Reckoning

The war ends, freezes into an unstable ceasefire, expands, or transforms into bloc competition.

The ending evaluates both:

- the condition of the world;
- the kind of person and economic actor the player became.

Personal wealth and world success are separate outcomes.

## Viable player identities

The game should support multiple defensible strategies rather than one dominant survival path.

Possible identities include:

- stable employee;
- entrepreneur;
- investor;
- public contractor;
- skilled professional;
- trader;
- community organizer;
- journalist or analyst;
- cross-border broker;
- smuggler or informal operator;
- wartime supplier;
- reformer;
- political insider;
- cautious saver.

Each route should offer opportunity, risk, social consequences, and a credible way to remain playable.

## Relationship architecture

Each adopted country should provide local equivalents of these recurring roles.

### Sponsor

Helped the player enter or settle and expects support, loyalty, work, repayment, or discretion.

### Local friend

Explains daily life and provides emotional connection to the adopted country.

### Rival immigrant

Begins from a similar position but follows a different economic and moral path.

### Employer, client, or investor

Creates opportunity and gradually introduces compromise, dependency, or obligation.

### Journalist or investigator

Connects personal events to the larger conflict and challenges unsupported claims.

### Community representative

Shows how economic decisions affect ordinary people, displaced residents, workers, and small businesses.

### Security or residency official

Controls movement, permissions, scrutiny, or status during escalation.

### Person from the former home

Maintains the player's connection to the life left behind and prevents immigration from becoming a cosmetic background choice.

## War design rules

War is experienced primarily through civilian and economic consequences.

The standard player is not automatically transformed into a battlefield commander.

War should affect:

- employment;
- wages;
- prices;
- shortages;
- rent and housing;
- insurance;
- lending;
- company performance;
- border access;
- remittances;
- residency status;
- propaganda and public trust;
- discrimination and social tension;
- infrastructure reliability;
- personal relationships;
- contract availability;
- legal and reputational exposure.

Combat may exist in the world and appear through reports, displacement, loss, infrastructure damage, or specific non-graphic narrative events. It should not replace the economic and immigrant perspective that makes Econovaria distinctive.

## Choice rule

Every major choice should answer three questions.

1. What can the player gain?
2. Who may be harmed, exposed, or alienated?
3. How can the decision return later?

Choices should express priorities and identity rather than a simple good-versus-evil meter.

Track consequences through concrete systems such as:

- wealth;
- liquidity;
- legal exposure;
- residency security;
- country reputation;
- institution trust;
- professional reputation;
- community standing;
- character relationships;
- access to contracts, routes, finance, and information.

## Ending families

Possible personal endings include:

- The Citizen;
- The Magnate;
- The Builder;
- The Broker;
- The Reformer;
- The Collaborator;
- The Exile;
- The Community Leader;
- The Survivor;
- The Stateless Financier.

Possible world endings include:

- negotiated peace;
- unstable ceasefire;
- divided economic blocs;
- one-country dominance;
- fragmented regional war;
- authoritarian stabilization;
- multilateral reconstruction;
- collapse of the Meridian project;
- reconstructed Corridor under new rules.

These are families, not final approved endings. Each requires clear trigger conditions and must avoid equating wealth with virtue.

## Content requirements

Every major event in the campaign should affect at least three layers:

1. player life;
2. economic systems;
3. political or institutional state.

Every major act should include:

- one personal objective;
- one economic opportunity;
- one relationship development;
- one world-state escalation or recovery;
- one delayed consequence;
- one meaningful player expression choice.

## Technical boundaries

This premise does not establish current runtime support for:

- player backgrounds;
- residency status;
- relationship state;
- personal obligations;
- war-state persistence;
- branching campaign state;
- military service;
- citizenship decisions;
- cross-border movement restrictions.

Until implemented and verified, these remain authored definitions and planned capabilities. Existing player identity, game scope, ledger, country assignment, and session authorization remain authoritative.

## Validation

Before staging:

- the player begins with personal stakes before the war;
- immigration affects opportunities and relationships rather than only introductory copy;
- no country is inherently good, evil, civilized, or criminal;
- war is not presented as a simple path to heroism or wealth;
- civilian consequences remain visible;
- multiple economic strategies remain viable;
- early choices return through later consequences;
- the player cannot control national policy without earned and mechanically supported authority;
- uncertainty and news correction remain meaningful;
- personal and world endings are evaluated separately;
- unsupported mechanics remain clearly marked as planned.

# World State and Historical Timeline

Status: draft foundation
Narrative epoch: Meridian Transition and Fracture
Primary player lens: new immigrant building a life in an adopted country

## Purpose

Define the shared historical background and starting conditions for a new Econovaria game session.

This file separates:

- durable world canon;
- variable scenario conditions;
- the player's personal starting position;
- game-session events and outcomes.

No historical or narrative definition in this document directly mutates runtime economic, player, or war state.

## Durable world premise

Econovaria is a network of ten specialized countries whose prosperity depends on trade, finance, technology, food, energy, industrial production, shipping, and data exchange.

No country can maintain modern living standards alone.

- Northreach controls strategic minerals, energy, and northern access but depends on technology, food, and foreign capital.
- Solvend creates advanced systems and research but depends on imported resources, skilled labor, and continued funding.
- Yrethia provides regulated trade, customs, shipping, and insurance but depends on open routes and global demand.
- Thaloris provides repair, overflow routing, re-export trade, and flexible logistics but depends on foreign recognition and insurer tolerance.
- Eldoran anchors food and commodity distribution but remains exposed to harvests, transport capacity, and inflation.
- Valerion supplies clean energy, water expertise, and premium services but depends on water availability, public confidence, and environmental credibility.
- Lumenor supplies diplomacy, education, media, and institutional legitimacy but depends on public trust and international participation.
- Xalvoria supplies capital and infrastructure finance but is exposed to defaults, political backlash, and concentrated influence.
- Dravenlok supplies steel, machinery, rail, and heavy construction but depends on energy, food, advanced components, and export access.
- Syndalis supplies cybersecurity, financial technology, and data infrastructure but depends on trust, regulation, and uninterrupted network access.

The central world rule is that every country's greatest strength is also a source of vulnerability.

## Player premise

The player arrives in one of the ten countries as a new immigrant.

They have limited money, temporary legal status, one local contact, and a reason to succeed. Depending on the selected or assigned background, the player may be seeking employment, education, safety, business opportunity, family reunification, political refuge, or access to the Meridian economic boom.

The player begins without political authority or established status. Their first concerns are personal:

- find work;
- pay rent;
- build savings;
- establish trust;
- improve residency status;
- support someone connected to the former home;
- create a future in the adopted country.

The global story is experienced through the consequences it creates in the player's life.

## Historical eras

### Era I: Consolidation of the Ten States

Regional authorities consolidated borders, taxation, transport, currencies, and national institutions.

Key developments:

- domestic rail, port, energy, water, and communications systems expanded;
- national currencies replaced fragmented regional instruments;
- capitals became administrative and commercial centers;
- governments adopted different approaches to ownership, trade, public services, and finance;
- sovereignty became central to national identity.

Legacy:

- strong national institutions;
- country-specific regulation;
- different infrastructure ownership models;
- persistent concern over foreign control.

### Era II: The Interdependence Acceleration

Cross-border specialization produced rapid growth.

Key developments:

- Yrethian port modernization reduced trade costs;
- Eldoran wholesale markets became regional pricing references;
- Xalvorian finance funded cross-border infrastructure;
- Solvend research increased the value of Northreach minerals;
- Dravenlok production expanded through regional supply chains;
- Valerion exported energy and water expertise;
- Lumenor became the preferred location for agreements and arbitration;
- Syndalis built financial and data-routing infrastructure;
- Thaloris expanded repair, bonded storage, and re-export capacity.

Legacy:

- multinational supply chains;
- cross-country company exposure;
- high trade-route sensitivity;
- shared infrastructure dependency;
- large movements of workers, students, and entrepreneurs between countries.

### Era III: The First Systems Shock

A combined period of shipping disruption, commodity inflation, financial stress, and cyber mistrust exposed the weakness of the interconnected system.

Key developments:

- Eldoran food prices rose after poor harvests and transport bottlenecks;
- Northreach restricted selected strategic exports;
- Yrethian insurance costs increased;
- Thaloris gained trade volume but faced stronger scrutiny;
- Dravenlok expanded industrial support;
- Xalvoria restructured several foreign loans;
- Syndalis faced a major trust crisis after a routing-disclosure controversy;
- Lumenor coordinated a temporary stabilization agreement.

Legacy:

- strategic reserves;
- stronger customs controls;
- public skepticism toward dependence;
- demand for more resilient infrastructure;
- political pressure to distinguish trusted and untrusted foreign actors.

### Era IV: The Quiet Recovery

The ten countries avoided systemic collapse but did not resolve the underlying tensions.

Key developments:

- growth returned unevenly;
- public and private debt increased;
- technology investment accelerated;
- housing and cost-of-living pressure grew in successful cities;
- companies shortened selected supply chains;
- infrastructure maintenance lagged;
- governments welcomed skilled immigrants while increasing security screening;
- international cooperation became more procedural and less trusted.

Legacy:

- positive but fragile growth;
- demand for workers and investment;
- political sensitivity around migration and foreign ownership;
- unresolved infrastructure backlogs;
- competing ideas of national resilience.

### Era V: The Meridian Boom

The proposed Meridian Corridor becomes the largest coordinated economic project since the First Systems Shock.

The Corridor is designed to connect:

- ports;
- rail;
- energy;
- water systems;
- customs;
- payment verification;
- data routes;
- commodity terminals;
- industrial facilities;
- research and maintenance systems.

The proposal creates a visible boom before construction is fully approved.

Governments, banks, contractors, logistics companies, manufacturers, technology firms, and universities begin hiring and positioning themselves for the expected investment.

Workers and immigrants move toward opportunity.

The player's standard campaign begins during this period.

### Era VI: The Meridian Fracture

Economic rivalry hardens around ownership, debt, data access, labor, environmental rules, migration, strategic materials, and national control.

The world does not enter war immediately.

The fracture develops through:

- trade restrictions;
- export reviews;
- port congestion;
- food and energy pressure;
- cyber incidents;
- disputed cargo records;
- hostile speeches;
- alliance commitments;
- military procurement;
- scrutiny of foreign workers and investors;
- capital flight and inventory stockpiling.

The player experiences these developments as changing prices, contracts, employment, residency procedures, relationships, and investment opportunities.

### Era VII: The Meridian Attack and War

A coordinated attack damages a major Meridian transport, customs, payment, or data node during a high-value shipment.

The attack causes civilian harm, infrastructure damage, cargo loss, payment disruption, emergency controls, and conflicting accusations.

Attribution is not immediately certain.

Retaliation, alliance pressure, border incidents, mobilization, and economic restrictions may turn the crisis into open war.

The standard campaign treats war as a civilian, economic, and social crisis rather than automatically making the player a military commander.

War-state outcomes remain session-specific.

## Default starting-world condition

The following describes narrative direction, not approved final numeric values.

### Global economy

- Growth: positive but slowing.
- Inflation: above preferred levels, driven by housing, food, logistics, and selected industrial inputs.
- Employment: expanding in strategic sectors, uneven elsewhere.
- Interest rates: moderately restrictive.
- Investment: strong in Corridor-related technology, logistics, finance, and construction.
- Confidence: optimistic in boom regions but increasingly cautious.
- Trade: high volume with limited spare capacity.
- Energy: stable overall with localized water and transmission risk.
- Food: adequate supply with rising price pressure.
- Finance: liquid but selective.
- Cyber risk: elevated.
- Migration: increasing toward countries and cities expected to benefit from the Corridor.

### Political economy

- Governments want growth but fear dependency.
- Businesses want fast approval and predictable access.
- Labor groups want jobs, wage protection, and fair treatment of migrant workers.
- Environmental institutions want binding safeguards.
- Security institutions want control over strategic data and resources.
- Smaller countries want access without domination.
- Opposition movements use foreign ownership, migration, debt, and security as political issues.
- Immigrants may be welcomed economically while remaining socially or legally vulnerable.

## Standard player opening

The player arrives before the war is publicly inevitable.

The standard opening should establish:

- adopted country;
- immigration background;
- temporary status;
- starting housing;
- starting balance;
- one sponsor or local contact;
- one work or business opportunity;
- one relationship to the former home;
- one immediate financial obligation;
- one long-term ambition.

The player should experience hope and opportunity before the world escalates.

## Default session-opening tensions

A new game may begin with all or a controlled subset of these tensions:

1. Meridian Corridor negotiations begin in Starfall.
2. Employers compete for skilled and migrant labor.
3. Housing costs rise in expected Corridor hubs.
4. Eldoran lowers its harvest forecast.
5. Sableport reports capacity pressure.
6. Northreach reviews strategic-mineral exports.
7. Solvend faces a skilled-labor shortage.
8. Valerion reservoir levels fall below seasonal expectations.
9. Xalvoria offers rapid financing with asset-control clauses.
10. Dravenlok requests guaranteed industrial procurement.
11. Syndalis warns of attacks against customs infrastructure.
12. Thaloris proposes emergency routing through Dusk Harbor.
13. Foreign residents face additional documentation checks.
14. Strategic companies begin stockpiling materials.
15. Public rhetoric becomes more openly hostile between rival blocs.

These are content triggers. A runtime session may activate, delay, omit, or vary them under an approved scenario profile.

## War escalation sequence

The standard authored sequence should use escalation rather than immediate catastrophe.

### Phase 1: Warning

- contradictory news;
- limited trade restrictions;
- isolated cyber failures;
- public demonstrations;
- military procurement increases;
- residency and travel reviews.

### Phase 2: Spark

- Meridian attack;
- civilian losses;
- damaged infrastructure;
- uncertain attribution;
- emergency controls;
- market and currency shock.

### Phase 3: Escalation

- retaliation;
- alliance activation;
- border closures;
- sanctions and asset restrictions;
- mobilization;
- propaganda;
- displacement.

### Phase 4: Sustained conflict

- rationing and shortages;
- strategic contracts;
- reconstruction needs;
- labor-market disruption;
- migration pressure;
- informal trade;
- loyalty tests;
- political consolidation or opposition.

### Phase 5: Reckoning

- negotiated peace;
- unstable ceasefire;
- divided blocs;
- regional conflict;
- authoritarian stabilization;
- reconstruction;
- continued low-level war.

The exact outcome remains game-session specific.

## Scenario profiles

### Standard immigrant campaign

- player begins as a new immigrant;
- Meridian boom creates early opportunity;
- tension escalates gradually;
- war occurs after personal stakes are established;
- multiple economic survival paths remain viable;
- civilian and economic consequences remain central.

### Market-intensive campaign

- stronger company, commodity, currency, and investment effects;
- more volatile boom and war markets;
- reduced narrative guidance;
- higher financial opportunity and risk.

### Policy-intensive campaign

- stronger institution and country decisions;
- more visible residency and public-policy effects;
- slower market effects;
- greater reputation and legitimacy consequences.

### Crisis-response campaign

- faster escalation;
- emergency contracts;
- constrained resources;
- shorter decision windows;
- explicit stabilization objectives.

### Peaceful sandbox

- war arc optional or disabled;
- broad economic content access;
- instructor-defined conditions;
- Meridian may remain a negotiation and development story.

## Continuity requirements

- Historical facts do not change between sessions unless the content-pack version changes.
- Starting conditions may vary by scenario profile.
- Player background, relationships, residency, active events, war state, and outcomes are session-specific.
- A war or peace outcome in one session never changes another session.
- News must distinguish history, current condition, forecast, allegation, attribution, and confirmed outcome.
- Characters must not refer to war as inevitable before the relevant runtime stage.
- Characters must not treat attribution as confirmed before the state records confirmation.
- Immigration background must affect authored opportunities or relationships to justify its presence.
- Follow-up arcs must reference the actual personal and world outcome rather than a presumed canonical ending.

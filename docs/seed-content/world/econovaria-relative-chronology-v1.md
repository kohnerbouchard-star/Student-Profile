## Purpose

Provide consistent chronological anchors for country lore, institutional history, company development, recurring characters, and story arcs without tying Econovaria to a real-world calendar.

## Calendar convention

The first classroom game session begins in **Year 100 of the Common Exchange Era**, abbreviated **CEE 100**.

The date is fictional. It exists only to maintain continuity.

Recommended display:

- formal records: CEE 100;
- student-facing recent history: “this year,” “three years ago,” or “during the First Systems Shock”;
- avoid exact day-and-month dates unless a scenario requires scheduling.

## Era structure

### Before CEE 0: Regional Exchange Period

Countries trade through separate bilateral systems. Ports, resources, food markets, financial centers, and industrial regions develop independently.

Continuity use:

- ancient or foundational country history;
- no current recurring character should have direct experience of this era.

### CEE 0: Common Exchange Convention

A group of trade, finance, and public institutions creates shared commercial standards for cross-border contracts, customs records, and currency-pair settlement.

Consequences:

- greater trade volume;
- growth of Yrethian insurance and Lumenorian arbitration;
- stronger demand for Xalvorian finance;
- gradual development of session and market structures later associated with global settlement.

### CEE 12–24: Infrastructure Expansion

Rail, port, energy, and industrial investment accelerates.

Country relevance:

- Dravenlok expands heavy manufacturing;
- Xalvoria develops long-term infrastructure finance;
- Eldoran becomes the central commodity and food-distribution anchor;
- Valerion expands water and hydropower systems.

### CEE 28: Frostgate Corridor Opening

Northreach completes the first reliable northern shipping and logistics system around Frostgate.

Consequences:

- strategic mineral exports expand;
- Solvend gains access to new materials;
- northern infrastructure and defense importance increases.

### CEE 34–41: Research Acceleration

Solvend universities, aerospace firms, and precision manufacturers develop internationally important systems and licensing models.

Consequences:

- greater demand for Northreach inputs;
- new talent mobility;
- stronger intellectual-property disputes;
- early collaboration with Syndalian data infrastructure.

### CEE 46: Sableport Modernization Compact

Yrethia completes a major port-automation and customs-standard program.

Consequences:

- higher throughput;
- stronger insurance and freight finance;
- rising concern over labor adjustment and port concentration;
- greater competitive tension with Thaloris.

### CEE 51–55: Dusk Harbor Recognition Crisis

A series of cargo-record and insurance disputes reduces Thaloris access to several regulated markets.

Consequences:

- creation of domestic trade-reform institutions;
- growth of bonded, repair, and secondary-market specialization;
- continuing disagreement over proportional regulation.

Continuity rule:

This event must not be described as proof that all Thalorian commerce is illegal.

### CEE 58: Crescent Bay Commodity Reform

Eldoran introduces more transparent commodity reporting, warehouse verification, and reserve review after a period of food-price volatility.

Consequences:

- stronger market credibility;
- continuing tension between producer income and household affordability;
- increased importance of logistics data.

### CEE 63–68: Green Infrastructure Cycle

Valerion expands hydropower, clean transit, water systems, and green finance.

Consequences:

- higher infrastructure quality;
- stronger environmental reputation;
- rising costs and access concerns;
- greater exposure to drought and greenwashing allegations.

### CEE 70: Starfall Institutional Charter

Lumenor reorganizes its conference, arbitration, public-record, and media institutions under stronger transparency and correction standards.

Consequences:

- increased diplomatic legitimacy;
- higher expectations for public process;
- greater criticism when decisions are slow.

### CEE 74–78: Ironhold Production Surge

Dravenlok expands state-backed steel, rail, vehicle, and heavy-equipment output.

Consequences:

- employment and export growth;
- rising energy use and debt;
- maintenance and modernization backlog;
- increased dependency on imported advanced components.

### CEE 80: Blacklight Network Consolidation

Syndalis becomes a major regional data, fintech, cybersecurity, and undersea-network center.

Consequences:

- lower digital transaction cost;
- strong security and data demand;
- concerns over concentration, surveillance, and foreign dependence.

### CEE 83–85: First Systems Shock

A combination of payment disruption, shipping delays, incompatible records, and institutional mistrust causes a temporary international trade slowdown.

Confirmed historical lessons:

- digital and physical infrastructure can fail together;
- manual fallback preserves some activity but at high cost;
- delayed attribution can become a diplomatic problem;
- overcentralization and fragmentation create different risks.

Continuity rule:

The shock must not be attributed retrospectively to one country without an approved canon decision.

### CEE 86–91: Quiet Recovery

Countries restore trade through smaller bilateral reforms, network redundancy, stronger insurance standards, and targeted infrastructure upgrades.

Consequences:

- growth returns;
- structural dependence remains;
- countries postpone a full shared-corridor agreement.

### CEE 92–96: Capital and Capacity Expansion

Xalvorian project finance, Dravenlok manufacturing, Solvend systems, and Yrethian logistics support a new investment cycle.

Consequences:

- higher infrastructure demand;
- growing project concentration;
- pressure on labor, components, and resources;
- stronger debate about ownership and debt.

### CEE 97: Consecutive Supply Warnings

Food, energy, and strategic-input forecasts become less stable.

Consequences:

- Eldoran increases reserve monitoring;
- Valerion reviews water and power commitments;
- Northreach reviews strategic exports;
- import-dependent countries seek redundancy.

### CEE 98: Meridian Feasibility Study

Lumenorian, Yrethian, Solvendian, and Xalvorian institutions publish separate studies proposing more integrated infrastructure.

The studies agree that current systems are inefficient but disagree about governance, financing, data, and standards.

### CEE 99: Preliminary Meridian Negotiations

Technical and diplomatic teams create the initial Corridor concept.

Unresolved questions:

- financing;
- ownership;
- voting;
- route selection;
- labor and environmental standards;
- data access;
- emergency control.

### CEE 100: Standard Game Opening

The Starfall Meridian Forum announces the first formal shared session.

Starting conditions:

- positive but slowing growth;
- moderate inflation pressure;
- high infrastructure demand;
- elevated trade volume;
- restrictive but not crisis-level rates;
- unresolved capacity and security concerns;
- no final Corridor agreement.

## Character-age and career continuity

Recommended recurring-character career ranges at CEE 100:

- senior institution leads: 12–30 years of professional experience;
- supporting experts and auditors: 8–22 years;
- early-career technical or workforce delegates: 2–8 years;
- no lead character should be credited with founding an institution before a plausible career start;
- biographies should reference eras and events rather than exact age unless age is relevant and reviewed.

## Company continuity

Company files should record:

- founding or predecessor period;
- major expansion period;
- current strategic role;
- whether the company existed during the First Systems Shock;
- historical contracts or failures that affect present reputation.

Do not imply current companies were unchanged across many decades. Use predecessors, mergers, or reorganizations only as narrative metadata until corporate-action mechanics exist.

## Story-arc chronology rules

- the Meridian pilot begins in CEE 100;
- country development arcs may reference conditions from CEE 97–99;
- the First Systems Shock is historical precedent, not the same event as the new customs-security intrusion;
- news must not say the Corridor has operated for years before the CEE 100 launch decision;
- a terminal Corridor outcome occurs during CEE 100 unless an extended scenario explicitly advances the year;
- follow-up arcs may occur later in CEE 100 or CEE 101.

## Correction and canon rules

- changing a historical anchor requires review of every dependent character, company, event, and institution;
- applied game-session history does not rewrite global chronology;
- custom scenarios may branch from CEE 100 but must label alternate history;
- player decisions cannot change events that occurred before the game-session start;
- a correction should distinguish an inaccurate historical claim from an alternate scenario.

## Validation

- every institution founding reference is chronologically plausible;
- every character career reference fits the timeline;
- company histories do not require unsupported corporate actions;
- current events do not duplicate historical events without explanation;
- student-facing copy uses consistent era names;
- no real-world year or direct national analogy is required;
- chronology remains global canon, while game-session outcomes remain session scoped.

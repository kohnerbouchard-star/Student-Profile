- pronunciation clarity;
- visual and phonetic distinction;
- accidental family implications;
- repeated naming patterns;
- country-level coherence;
- classroom readability;
- avoidance of direct real-world cultural coding.

This review occurs before character names are treated as production canon.

## Pattern findings

### High-risk collision group 1: Vale

- Neris Vale
- Eren Vale

Risk:

Students may assume a family relationship. Spoken discussion cannot distinguish the surnames.

Required action:

Rename one character or explicitly establish a meaningful family relationship. A coincidental exact surname is not recommended.

Recommended change:

- retain Neris Vale;
- rename Eren Vale to a distinct Eldoran name.

Working replacement candidate:

- Eren Calder — EH-ren KAL-der.

### High-risk collision group 2: Meren and Merin

- Ila Meren
- Sola Merin

Risk:

The names are visually and phonetically close while the characters occupy different countries and institutions.

Recommended change:

- retain Ila Meren;
- rename Sola Merin.

Working replacement candidate:

- Talia Quen — TAH-lee-ah KWEN.

### High-risk collision group 3: Sol and Sola

- Dena Sol
- Sola Merin
- country name Solvend

Risk:

The names create an unintended shared lexical pattern across Lumenor, Valerion, and Solvend.

Recommended change:

- rename Sola Merin as above;
- rename Dena Sol.

Working replacement candidate:

- Dena Holt — DEH-nah HOHLT.

### Moderate collision group 4: Sena and Sera

- Sena Oris
- Sera Noll

Risk:

Names differ by one consonant and may be confused in spoken classroom discussion.

Options:

1. Retain both but always show country, portrait, and institution.
2. Rename Sera Noll because the supporting character is less deeply referenced.
3. Rename Sena Oris only if the lead Solvend identity changes.

Recommended change:

- retain Sena Oris;
- consider changing Sera Noll to Maelin Noll — MAY-lin NOHL.

### Moderate collision group 5: Mara, Mira, Nara, Amara

- Mira Sen
- Mara Volsk
- Nara Esen
- Amara Tey

Risk:

All use similar open-vowel structures and may blur during rapid classroom dialogue.

Assessment:

Surnames and country roles are sufficiently distinct in written content, but audio-only or spoken use may still cause confusion.

Recommendation:

- retain Mira Sen and Mara Volsk;
- consider changing Nara Esen or Amara Tey if voice testing shows confusion;
- always display institution and country with character messages.

## Country naming-style observations

The current names are intentionally fictional and not one-to-one copies of real national naming systems.

This is appropriate for avoiding direct real-world cultural stereotyping.

However, the current set does not yet establish strong country-specific naming conventions. Production options:

### Option A: Global mixed naming pool

Characters across countries use a shared fictional naming environment.

Benefits:

- avoids essentializing countries through names;
- supports migration and mixed backgrounds;
- simpler content production.

Costs:

- weaker country flavor;
- greater collision risk;
- less implicit geographic context.

### Option B: Country-specific phonetic tendencies

Each country uses light phonetic patterns without copying a real language.

Benefits:

- stronger identity;
- easier contextual recognition.

Costs:

- greater risk of stereotyping or overconstructed fantasy naming;
- requires a formal naming guide.

### Option C: Institution and city carry identity, not the personal name

Personal names remain globally varied. Country and institution are always shown.

Recommended.

This supports a grounded international setting and reduces pressure to make every fictional name signal nationality.

## Production naming rules

1. Character cards and interactions always show name, role, institution, and country.
2. No two recurring characters share an exact surname unless a relationship is intentional and documented.
3. Avoid two names differing by only one sound when both appear in the same arc.
4. Avoid more than three recurring names beginning with the same first syllable in one pilot.
5. Provide pronunciation guidance for names with non-obvious spellings.
6. Do not encode moral alignment through harsh or soft naming patterns.
7. Do not use names strongly associated with a single real-world culture merely as fictional country shorthand.
8. Search current and planned characters before approving a new name.
9. A display-name change during draft maturity may change the stable ID before canon lock.
10. After canon approval, display-name changes require alias and migration handling rather than silent replacement.

## Reference-update requirements

A draft rename must update:

- character filename;
- stable content ID;
- institution lead-character reference;
- country design file;
- student country briefing;
- related news;
- interaction or event references;
- catalog index;
- any review or reaction matrix;
- future asset filenames and alt text.

Do not create aliases until a name has been exposed in a released content pack.
